## Overview

**Adult neurogenesis** is the generation of new neurons in the brain after development is complete. In rodents and many other mammals, two niches reliably produce new neurons throughout life: the subgranular zone of the dentate gyrus (SGZ, hippocampus) and the subventricular zone (SVZ, lining the lateral ventricles). The dentate gyrus pathway integrates new granule cells into the trisynaptic hippocampal circuit and has been implicated in pattern separation, memory, and mood regulation. Whether the same process operates in adult humans has become one of the most contested empirical questions in modern neuroscience.

## How It Works (in rodents)

Quiescent radial glia-like neural stem cells in the SGZ asymmetrically divide, producing intermediate progenitors that proliferate, differentiate into immature neurons (expressing markers DCX, PSA-NCAM, calretinin), migrate the short distance into the granule cell layer, mature over weeks, and eventually integrate into hippocampal circuits as functional dentate granule cells. New granule cells exhibit heightened excitability and plasticity during a 4-6 week "critical period" before adopting mature properties.

Functionally, manipulations that suppress adult neurogenesis impair certain forms of pattern separation, contextual fear discrimination, and antidepressant responses. Manipulations that boost it (exercise, environmental enrichment, antidepressants) often improve them.

## Key Findings (rodents — well established)

- **Continuous SGZ neurogenesis throughout life** in rats, mice, and many other species (Altman & Das 1965; Kaplan & Hinds 1977; Eriksson et al. 1998).
- **Pattern separation role:** New granule cells help distinguish similar contexts (Clelland et al. 2009; Sahay et al. 2011).
- **Activity-dependence:** Running, environmental enrichment, and learning increase neurogenesis; stress and ageing reduce it.
- **Antidepressant link:** Many antidepressants increase hippocampal neurogenesis, and some of their behavioural effects in mice depend on it (Santarelli et al. 2003).

## Key Findings (humans — disputed)

- **Eriksson et al. 1998:** BrdU-labelled new neurons were found in the dentate gyrus of cancer patients who had received BrdU diagnostically. The first direct evidence for adult human neurogenesis.
- **Spalding et al. 2013:** ¹⁴C bomb-pulse dating of post-mortem hippocampi inferred ~700 new neurons added per day per dentate gyrus, persisting into old age.
- **Sorrells et al. 2018:** Found *no* DCX+ immature neurons in adult human dentate gyrus across a 59-subject sample; argued that adult human hippocampal neurogenesis is undetectable ([[source-sorrells-2018|paper]]).
- **Boldrini et al. 2018:** In a 28-subject sample using different fixation and antibodies, found *thousands* of immature neurons in the dentate gyrus persisting to age 79 ([[source-boldrini-2018|paper]]).
- **Moreno-Jiménez et al. 2019** (*Nature Medicine*): Reported abundant immature neurons in healthy adult human dentate gyrus and a sharp decline in Alzheimer's, supporting persistence.
- **Franjic et al. 2022** (*Neuron*): Single-nucleus RNA-seq found little molecular signature of active neurogenesis in adult human dentate gyrus, supporting the Sorrells side.

## Recent Developments

- **The methodological dispute:** The dispute now appears to hinge on tissue handling. Post-mortem interval, fixation method, and especially antibody choice and concentration produce quantitatively different DCX-staining results. The Boldrini protocol uses freshly fixed, intact hippocampi from a controlled tissue bank; the Sorrells sample mixes sources. Replication studies in 2026 lean toward "low-rate but real" rather than "absent".
- **Functional implications still open:** Even if adult human neurogenesis exists, its rate is dramatically lower than rodents'. Whether it contributes meaningfully to human cognition, mood, or memory is unsettled.
- **Alzheimer's connection:** Multiple groups now report that hippocampal neurogenesis declines with Alzheimer's pathology, both in human tissue and in mouse models — making neurogenesis a candidate substrate for the early memory loss that defines the disease.
- **Other niches:** Adult neurogenesis has been claimed in striatum, hypothalamus, and cortex but these claims are even more disputed than the dentate one.

## Connections

Adult hippocampal neurogenesis sits at the intersection of [[memory-consolidation|Memory Consolidation]] (new granule cells modulate hippocampal output during consolidation), [[episodic-memory|Episodic Memory]] (pattern separation supports event-distinct memory), and basic [[neuron|neuron]] cell biology. It overlaps with the [[memory-engrams|Memory Engrams]] literature: new neurons may preferentially join recent engrams, providing a substrate for time-stamped memory.

For VaultMind, the take-away is methodological more than mechanistic. When two carefully run experiments produce opposite results, the right move is to identify the moderator (here: tissue handling, antibody, post-mortem interval) rather than picking a side. The same lesson applies to retrieval evals where benchmark-construction details swing the answer — the substrate / data-handling matters as much as the algorithm.
