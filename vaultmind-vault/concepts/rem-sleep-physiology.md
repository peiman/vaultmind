## Overview

**REM (rapid eye movement) sleep** is the brain state in which EEG desynchronises to a waking-like pattern while the body is in near-complete skeletal muscle atonia. It was discovered by Aserinsky and Kleitman (1953) and was immediately associated with dreaming. The paradox of a "waking brain in a sleeping body" — coined "paradoxical sleep" by Jouvet — arises from an unusual neuromodulator profile: acetylcholine (ACh) is high (as in waking), but noradrenaline (NA) and serotonin (5-HT) are near-zero. This profile has deep functional consequences for memory.

REM sleep constitutes roughly 20–25% of total sleep in healthy adults, increasing in proportion across the night so that the final 90-minute cycle is almost entirely REM. Hippocampal **theta rhythm** (4–8 Hz) is prominent during REM, driven by the medial septum — the same oscillation that organises spatial coding during active waking behaviour. REM is also when the **PGO (ponto-geniculo-occipital) wave** system activates, generating endogenous bursts of activity from the brainstem to visual cortex.

## How It Works

**Neuromodulator profile:** The combination of high ACh and near-zero NA/5-HT during REM is the opposite of slow-wave sleep (low ACh, moderate NA/5-HT). ACh promotes hippocampal theta and facilitates synaptic plasticity; the absence of NA removes the gating that would otherwise suppress "off-line" associative processing. This environment is thought to be permissive for broad, cross-domain associative processes rather than the precise, hippocampus→cortex write operations of NREM.

**PGO waves:** Pontine reticular neurons fire phasic bursts (PGO waves) that propagate from the pons to the lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN) to visual cortex, generating the rapid eye movements and much of the visual imagery of dreaming. PGO waves activate large swaths of visual association cortex and are thought to drive the random, quasi-hallucinatory content of dreams. They may also serve a developmental role — PGO waves are far more frequent in neonates (who spend ~50% of sleep in REM) and may drive visual system maturation.

**Hippocampal theta:** The medial septum drives theta oscillations in the hippocampus during both REM sleep and active waking. During REM, theta is prominent even though the animal is not locomoting, suggesting that the hippocampus is engaged in some form of internal processing — potentially the associative consolidation and emotional tagging that REM is thought to serve.

**Atonia mechanism:** Glycinergic and GABAergic interneurons in the medullary reticular formation hyperpolarise spinal motor neurons during REM, preventing the motor commands generated in the (paradoxically active) motor cortex from reaching muscles. Failure of this mechanism causes REM sleep behaviour disorder (RBD), where patients act out dreams.

## Key Findings

- **REM and procedural memory:** Late-night sleep, which is REM-rich, preferentially benefits motor sequence learning and perceptual skills relative to early-night SWS-rich sleep ([[source-plihal-born-1997|Plihal & Born 1997]]).
- **Emotional memory consolidation:** [[source-walker-2009|Walker (2009)]] reviews evidence that REM sleep specifically consolidates emotional memories — enhancing memory for the emotional content of experiences while "stripping" associated physiological arousal ("sleep to forget, sleep to remember" hypothesis).
- **REM and insight/creativity:** Wagner et al. (2004) showed that sleep — particularly REM-dominated late sleep — dramatically increases the probability of discovering a hidden mathematical shortcut, suggesting REM promotes the kind of cross-domain insight that requires loosening of semantic constraints.
- **Selective cholinergic environment:** The ACh-high, NA-low environment of REM may specifically support backward-propagating (cortex→hippocampus) information flow, in contrast to NREM which supports forward (hippocampus→cortex) flow — a directional switching hypothesis proposed by Hasselmo and colleagues.

## Recent Developments

- **REM and fear extinction:** Cholinergic activity during REM appears critical for consolidating extinction of conditioned fear (the basis of exposure therapy). Sleep disruption after trauma may impair extinction consolidation, contributing to PTSD maintenance.
- **REM deprivation effects:** Selective REM deprivation (without disrupting NREM) impairs procedural and emotional memory but leaves declarative memory relatively intact — stage dissociation evidence supporting distinct functional roles.
- **Theta coupling:** Recent work shows that during REM, hippocampal theta is coupled to gamma oscillations in the medial prefrontal cortex, suggesting a replay mechanism distinct from the SWR-based NREM replay — possibly supporting schema abstraction rather than episode transfer.
- **REM sleep decreases with age:** REM percentage declines with normal ageing, paralleling declines in emotional processing and procedural learning. Whether REM reduction is a cause or consequence of these cognitive changes is an active research area.

## Connections

REM sleep physiology provides a distinct consolidation environment from NREM: where NREM is structured (hierarchical oscillatory coupling), REM is associative and exploratory (high ACh, theta, near-absent NA/5-HT). Together, the two stages constitute the full consolidation cycle described in the [[active-systems-consolidation|Active Systems Consolidation]] hypothesis — NREM for hippocampus→cortex transfer, REM for generalisation and emotional processing. The PGO system links REM to the [[default-mode-network|Default Mode Network]] patterns of spontaneous thought.

For VaultMind, the REM/NREM distinction maps to two kinds of background processing: **structured consolidation** (NREM — run the indexer, write explicit links) versus **associative enrichment** (REM — find unexpected cross-domain connections, generate candidate hypotheses from loose embedding proximity).
