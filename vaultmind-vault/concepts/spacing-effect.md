## Overview

The spacing effect is the finding that memory retention is better when learning sessions are distributed over time rather than massed together. Reviewing material at increasing intervals (spaced repetition) produces dramatically better long-term retention than cramming.

This is one of the most robust findings in cognitive psychology, replicated across materials, age groups, and modalities.

## Key Properties

- **Optimal spacing increases with retention interval:** Longer target retention → longer gaps between reviews
- **Desirable difficulty:** Spacing makes retrieval harder in the short term but stronger in the long term
- **Applies to all memory types:** Facts, skills, concepts, and procedural knowledge all benefit
- **Diminishing returns:** After sufficient spacing, additional reviews add less incremental retention

## Connections

While VaultMind is not a spaced repetition system, the spacing effect is relevant to how agents should interact with their memory. An agent that retrieves the same note repeatedly in quick succession gains less than one that retrieves it across different contexts and time periods. This supports the case for [[Base-Level Activation]] tracking — retrieval frequency matters, but so does the distribution of retrievals over time.
