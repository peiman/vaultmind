## Overview

Purkinje cells are large GABAergic neurons of the cerebellar cortex, named after the Czech anatomist Jan Evangelista Purkyně who described them in 1837. They are among the most morphologically distinctive neurons in the brain: a single soma in the Purkinje layer gives rise to an enormously elaborate, almost two-dimensional dendritic arbor that fans out perpendicular to the cerebellar folia. Purkinje cells are the *sole output* of the cerebellar cortex — every cerebellar computation is funneled through their axons to the deep cerebellar nuclei (and, for some, the vestibular nuclei).

## Anatomy & Function

- **Soma:** Large flask-shaped cell body, ~25 µm, located in a single monolayer (the Purkinje layer) between the granular layer and molecular layer.
- **Dendritic arbor:** Densely spinous, espalier-like branches confined to a near-planar sheet. A single Purkinje cell hosts on the order of 150,000 parallel-fiber synapses — among the highest synaptic counts of any neuron.
- **Axon:** Sole output of cerebellar cortex; GABAergic; projects to deep cerebellar nuclei (dentate, interposed, fastigial) and vestibular nuclei. By inhibiting these nuclei, Purkinje cells control all cerebellar output.
- **Two excitatory input streams:**
  - **Parallel fibers** (axons of cerebellar granule cells): each Purkinje cell receives ~150,000 weak parallel-fiber synapses on its spines. Encode contextual sensorimotor information.
  - **Climbing fibers** (axons from the inferior olive): a *single* climbing fiber wraps the proximal dendrites of each Purkinje cell with hundreds of strong synapses. Climbing-fiber activation produces a characteristic *complex spike* (a burst of fast and slower spikelets atop a calcium plateau).
- **Firing patterns:** *Simple spikes* (driven by parallel fibers, ~50-100 Hz tonic) and *complex spikes* (driven by climbing fibers, ~1 Hz). The complex spike is thought to convey an error or teaching signal.

## Connections

Purkinje cells are the central player in classical theories of cerebellar motor learning (Marr 1969, Albus 1971, Ito 1982): the climbing-fiber complex spike acts as a teaching signal that weakens recently active parallel-fiber synapses via long-term depression (LTD). This is a notable counterpoint to canonical [[hebbian-learning|Hebbian Learning]] in cortex and hippocampus — same Hebbian *form*, but with depression rather than potentiation as the dominant outcome, and with an explicit error-driven supervisor. Cerebellar circuits trained by this rule fine-tune the timing, gain, and coordination of movements that ultimately reach [[motor-neurons|Motor Neurons]] via descending pathways. Purkinje cell degeneration — from genetic ataxias, alcohol toxicity, paraneoplastic autoimmunity, or stroke — produces ataxia, dysmetria, and intention tremor.
