## Overview

This note focuses on the **sleep-specific aspects of glymphatic function** and extends the mechanistic account in [[concept-glymphatic-system|Glymphatic System]]. While the glymphatic network operates continuously, its throughput is dramatically upregulated during sleep — and specifically during slow-wave sleep (N3). The Xie et al. (2013) finding that interstitial space expands ~60% during sleep, accelerating amyloid-β and tau clearance, has been followed by work identifying the precise neural correlates of maximal glymphatic efficiency within the sleep period.

The key advance from [[source-hablitz-2019|Hablitz et al. (2019)]] was quantifying the within-sleep gradient: glymphatic influx (CSF inflow) is not uniform across sleep stages but is highest during periods of maximal EEG **delta power** (0.5–4 Hz), corresponding to deep slow-wave sleep. This directly links the electroencephalographic signature of SWS — which is independently known to be the stage of hippocampal replay — to the metabolic housekeeping that maintains the biochemical environment for plasticity.

## How It Works

During slow-wave sleep, several converging factors maximise glymphatic throughput. First, the interstitial space expands (as shown by Xie et al.) due to reduced noradrenergic tone (noradrenaline contracts astrocytes) and reduced synaptic swelling. Second, slow arterial pulsations — whose amplitude increases during slow-wave sleep — act as a pump driving paravascular CSF inflow. Third, AQP4 channels are most efficiently aligned to paravascular spaces during sleep postures and slow-wave states.

Hablitz et al. (2019) showed in mice that artificially increasing slow-wave EEG power (via optogenetics or chemogenetics to suppress noradrenaline release) proportionally increased glymphatic influx, while disrupting slow oscillations reduced it. The coupling is bidirectional: the same neural state that drives memory replay (slow-wave activity) also drives waste clearance, suggesting the two functions are co-regulated by a shared underlying mechanism — probably noradrenergic suppression.

## Key Findings

- **Delta power predicts glymphatic influx:** EEG delta power (slow-wave intensity) during sleep positively predicts simultaneous glymphatic tracer influx, measured by two-photon imaging ([[source-hablitz-2019|Hablitz et al. 2019]]).
- **Noradrenaline suppression is the shared switch:** Reducing noradrenaline (which promotes waking arousal) both increases SWA and increases glymphatic clearance — suggesting that slow-wave sleep is the shared upstream controller of both functions.
- **Low heart rate contributes:** Slow-wave sleep is also associated with lower heart rate and longer cardiac cycles; longer cardiac cycles create longer arterial pulsation periods, increasing perivascular pumping efficiency.
- **Human evidence:** Fultz et al. (2019, *Science*) used simultaneous fMRI and EEG in humans to show that slow oscillations in deep NREM sleep were coupled to large-amplitude, slow CSF pulsations through the aqueduct — the first direct human evidence for coupling between slow-wave activity and glymphatic-like CSF flow.
- **Amyloid-β clearance rates:** After one night of sleep deprivation in humans, CSF amyloid-β increased by ~30% (Holth et al. 2019, *Science*), consistent with impaired glymphatic clearance.

## Recent Developments

- **CSF oscillations in humans:** The Fultz et al. (2019) result — slow oscillations driving periodic CSF pulsations visible on fMRI — provides a non-invasive human measure of glymphatic activity, opening the door to clinical monitoring.
- **Noradrenaline microdoses:** Pharmacologically reducing locus coeruleus firing during sleep (e.g., with low-dose prazosin) increases SWA and glymphatic clearance in rodents, suggesting a therapeutic target for improving both sleep quality and waste clearance simultaneously.
- **Sleep fragmentation:** Even brief arousal events during NREM, which suppress SWA, reduce glymphatic clearance — suggesting that sleep continuity, not just total duration, is critical for effective waste removal.

## Connections

This concept extends [[concept-glymphatic-system|Glymphatic System]] by pinpointing the within-sleep dynamics: peak clearance during [[concept-slow-wave-activity|slow-wave activity]] means that the same oscillatory regime that drives [[concept-hippocampal-cortical-dialogue|hippocampal-cortical memory consolidation]] also drives waste clearance. Both processes are suppressed by the same manipulation — locus coeruleus activation, noradrenaline release, arousal — and both benefit from deep, undisturbed N3 sleep. Sleep deprivation effects on memory ([[concept-sleep-deprivation-memory|Sleep Deprivation and Memory]]) include both consolidation failure and clearance failure.
