## Overview

"Sleep hygiene" is the set of behavioral and environmental practices that support adequate, high-quality sleep. The term has been diluted by pop-science and wellness content, but a well-evidenced core of practices exists — practices with direct empirical support from controlled studies and clear mechanistic rationales. This note documents only what has meaningful evidence, not the broader wellness-industry canon.

## Temperature

Core body temperature must drop by ~1–2°C for sleep onset. Warmer room temperatures that prevent this drop increase sleep onset latency and reduce SWS. The optimal bedroom temperature for most adults is **16–19°C (60–67°F)** — considerably cooler than waking comfort temperature. Studies using water-circulating suits that actively cool the body show that thermal manipulation can advance sleep onset and increase SWS (Raymann et al., 2008, *Brain*; van den Heuvel et al., 2004). A warm bath or shower 1–2 hours before sleep paradoxically accelerates sleep onset by drawing blood to the skin surface, facilitating core cooling.

## Light and Darkness

**Pre-sleep**: As above (see [[presleep-behavior-encoding|Pre-Sleep Behaviors]]), light suppresses melatonin. Blackout curtains that produce complete darkness during sleep protect the final REM-rich sleep cycles from early morning light, which is particularly important for late chronotypes. Even dim light (~10 lux) during sleep has been shown to increase heart rate and blood glucose vs. sleeping in complete darkness (Mason et al., 2022, *PNAS*).

**Morning light**: Bright light exposure within 30 minutes of waking is the most effective circadian anchor — it sets the clock by signalling the biological day-start. For individuals trying to advance their circadian phase, morning light is the primary tool.

## Sleep Regularity

Regular sleep timing — same bed and wake time 7 days a week — is consistently associated with better sleep quality, mood, and cognitive performance across epidemiological studies. Irregular sleep schedules (large social jetlag) correlate with worse health outcomes independent of total sleep duration (Phillips et al., 2017). The mechanism is likely circadian entrainment: a consistent morning light signal at the same time each day produces a tightly entrained clock; irregular timing produces a chronically shifting, weakly entrained clock.

The "sleeping in on weekends to catch up" strategy extends sleep duration but preserves the social jetlag-like phase shift — Monday waking is more difficult, not easier.

## Regularity Evidence

Fairfield et al. (2019, *Scientific Reports*) showed that sleep regularity index (SRI) in university students predicted GPA better than average sleep duration or average sleep timing alone. This is a correlational finding but convergent with mechanistic evidence for circadian regularity.

## Exercise

Moderate aerobic exercise (but not intense exercise within 2–3 hours of sleep) consistently increases SWS depth and reduces sleep onset latency across multiple RCTs. Passos et al. (2011, *Sleep Medicine*) found that a single session of moderate-intensity exercise significantly improved polysomnographically measured sleep quality in patients with chronic insomnia. Regular exercisers have more slow-wave activity than sedentary controls.

## What Is NOT Well-Evidenced

- **Herbal supplements** (valerian, chamomile): RCT evidence is weak; effect sizes are small and inconsistent.
- **Melatonin for general sleep quality**: Exogenous melatonin is most effective as a *timing* signal, not a sedative. Its primary evidence base is for jet lag and delayed sleep phase disorder. For general sleep quality in non-shifted individuals, the evidence is unimpressive.
- **"Sleep-tracking" accuracy**: Consumer wearables can track sleep duration reasonably but misclassify sleep stages substantially. They may increase sleep anxiety ("orthosomnia") — worrying about sleep data rather than simply sleeping.

## Practical Implications

1. **Temperature**: Set bedroom to 16–18°C. Take a warm shower 90 minutes before bed to accelerate core cooling.
2. **Darkness**: Blackout curtains + no screen light during sleep. Use sunrise-simulating alarm for gentle waking.
3. **Consistency**: Same wake time every day, including weekends, is more important than same bedtime. The wake time anchors the circadian clock.
4. **Morning light**: Within 30 minutes of waking, get 10+ minutes of outdoor light or use a 10,000-lux light box.
5. **Exercise**: 150+ minutes/week of moderate aerobic activity, finishing >3 hours before bed.
6. **No alcohol as a sleep aid**: See [[presleep-behavior-encoding|Pre-Sleep Behaviors]].

## Connections

Sleep hygiene practices support the sleep architecture (SWS depth, REM completeness) that enables [[memory-consolidation|Memory Consolidation]], [[targeted-memory-reactivation|Targeted Memory Reactivation]], and [[closed-loop-sleep-stimulation|Closed-Loop Sleep Stimulation]] to function. The [[glymphatic-system|Glymphatic System]] is most active during SWS — deep, temperature-appropriate sleep maximises glymphatic clearance alongside memory consolidation.
