## Overview

Pyramidal neurons are the most numerous excitatory neurons in the mammalian cerebral cortex and the principal projection cells of the hippocampus and amygdala. They take their name from the conic, pyramid-shaped soma. Roughly 70-85% of cortical neurons are pyramidal, and they are responsible for nearly all long-range excitatory communication in the cortex — between cortical areas, between hemispheres (via the corpus callosum), and from cortex to subcortical targets such as striatum, thalamus, brainstem, and spinal cord.

Pyramidal cells are glutamatergic: they release glutamate at most synapses and depolarize their targets. Their excitation is balanced and shaped by local GABAergic [[interneurons|Interneurons]], producing the excitation-inhibition equilibrium on which stable cortical computation depends.

## Anatomy & Function

- **Soma:** Pyramid-shaped cell body, typically located in cortical layers 2/3, 5, or 6 (with layer 5 cells the largest, including Betz cells in primary motor cortex).
- **Apical dendrite:** A single thick dendrite ascending from the apex of the soma toward the pial surface, often branching into a tuft in layer 1. Receives top-down and contextual inputs.
- **Basal dendrites:** Multiple dendrites spreading laterally from the base, sampling local cortical inputs.
- **Dendritic spines:** Thousands per cell (~3,000 in rat; ~6,000 in human apical dendrite). Each spine hosts an excitatory synapse and is the site of most plasticity.
- **Axon:** A single axon descending from the soma; collateralizes locally and projects long-distance to other cortical areas, contralateral cortex, or subcortical structures.
- **Integration:** Each cell integrates ~30,000 excitatory and ~1,700 inhibitory inputs — a massive convergence that supports the rich representational capacity of cortex.

Pyramidal cells implement two-stage dendritic integration: distal apical inputs trigger calcium spikes that interact with somatic sodium spikes, providing a biological substrate for coincidence detection between feedforward (basal) and feedback (apical-tuft) information streams.

## Connections

Pyramidal neurons are the substrate where [[hebbian-learning|Hebbian Learning]] is most extensively studied: long-term potentiation (LTP) at Schaffer-collateral synapses onto CA1 pyramidal cells in the hippocampus is the canonical model preparation for synaptic plasticity. Their excitatory output is sculpted into precise temporal patterns by the three-class [[interneurons|Interneuron]] inhibitory network. Together with [[action-potential|Action Potentials]] and [[synaptic-transmission|Synaptic Transmission]], pyramidal-cell circuitry constitutes the cellular basis of cortical computation, working memory, and conscious perception.
