## Overview

Neurotransmitters are endogenous chemical messengers released by a presynaptic [[neuron|Neuron]] into the synaptic cleft to act on receptors of a postsynaptic cell. They are the molecular vocabulary of [[synaptic-transmission|Synaptic Transmission]] — but the same molecule can be excitatory at one synapse and inhibitory at another, because the postsynaptic effect is determined by the receptor, not the transmitter alone.

Functionally, transmitters fall into three broad roles:

- **Excitatory:** Net depolarizing effect on the postsynaptic cell (typically opens cation-selective ligand-gated [[ion-channels|Ion Channels]]).
- **Inhibitory:** Net hyperpolarizing or shunting effect (typically opens Cl- or K+ channels).
- **Modulatory:** Slower, longer-lasting effects via metabotropic G-protein-coupled receptors and intracellular second-messenger cascades. These rarely fire postsynaptic spikes on their own; instead they bias entire circuits' excitability and plasticity.

## Key Examples

- **Glutamate** — the dominant fast excitatory transmitter in the vertebrate CNS. Acts on AMPA, NMDA, and kainate ionotropic receptors plus metabotropic mGluRs. NMDA-receptor activation is the molecular trigger for most forms of LTP.
- **GABA (γ-aminobutyric acid)** — the dominant fast inhibitory transmitter in the brain. Acts on ionotropic GABA-A (Cl- channels, target of benzodiazepines and most general anesthetics) and metabotropic GABA-B receptors. In the spinal cord this role is shared with glycine.
- **Acetylcholine (ACh)** — the transmitter at the neuromuscular junction (nicotinic receptors, ionotropic) and a major modulatory transmitter in the CNS (muscarinic receptors, metabotropic). Cholinergic basal-forebrain projections gate cortical attention and arousal. The first neurotransmitter discovered (Loewi, 1921).
- **Dopamine** — modulatory monoamine. Midbrain projections (VTA → striatum, VTA → cortex; substantia nigra → dorsal striatum) carry reward-prediction-error signals, gate motor initiation, and shape goal-directed learning. Implicated in Parkinson's disease, addiction, schizophrenia.
- **Serotonin (5-HT)** — modulatory monoamine from the raphe nuclei. Broad projections regulate mood, sleep, appetite, thermoregulation. Target of SSRIs.
- **Norepinephrine (noradrenaline)** — modulatory monoamine from the locus coeruleus. Drives arousal, vigilance, the central component of the fight-or-flight response, and gain control on cortical processing.

Beyond these "classical" small-molecule transmitters there are dozens of neuropeptides (substance P, opioids, oxytocin, ...), purines (ATP, adenosine), and gasotransmitters (NO, CO).

## Connections

Neurotransmitters define the functional channels of [[synaptic-transmission|Synaptic Transmission]]: the same anatomical wiring diagram supports radically different computations depending on which transmitter and receptor combination is in use at each synapse. Glutamate-gated AMPA and NMDA receptors are the [[ion-channels|Ion Channels]] that carry most fast excitation; GABA-A receptors carry most fast inhibition. The slow, diffuse modulatory systems (DA, 5-HT, NE, ACh) are how the brain implements global state — arousal, mood, attention, learning rate — on top of the fast E/I core.
