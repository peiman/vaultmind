## Overview

The **AIM model** (Hobson, 1990s–2000s) is a three-dimensional state-space framework for characterising any brain-mind state in terms of three continuously varying parameters: **A**ctivation level, **I**nput source (internal vs. external), and **M**odulation (aminergic vs. cholinergic dominance). Rather than treating wake, NREM, and REM as discrete categories, AIM maps all brain-mind states — including psychosis, anaesthesia, hypnosis, and drug states — onto a continuous manifold. ([[source-hobson-2009|Hobson 2009]])

The model unified the activation-synthesis hypothesis by providing it with a mechanistic language: dreaming is not a binary state but a region in AIM space characterised by high activation (comparable to waking), internally generated input, and cholinergic/low-aminergic modulation.

## How It Works

**A — Activation:** The overall level of neuronal activity, measured as the EEG-spectral energy of the brain. Ranges from near-zero in deep NREM (stage N3) to the high sustained activation of both waking and REM, which are roughly equivalent in total cortical activation level.

**I — Input source:** The degree to which input to the cortex originates from the external world (sensory systems) versus internal brainstem/limbic circuits. During waking, I is maximally external; during REM, brainstem PGO waves dominate (maximally internal); during NREM, I is intermediate and low (both sources quiet).

**M — Modulation:** The balance between aminergic neurotransmitter systems (noradrenaline from locus coeruleus, serotonin from raphe nuclei — promoting attention, logic, self-monitoring) and cholinergic systems (ACh from pontine nuclei — promoting associative, bizarre imagery, amnesia, hallucination). In waking: high aminergic. In REM: high cholinergic / low aminergic. In NREM: both systems down-regulated.

The three-way combination explains why REM dreaming has waking-level vividness (A high), hallucinatory quality (I internal), and poor metacognition with emotional, associative thinking (M cholinergic): each dimension contributes independently.

## Key Findings

- **Lucid dreaming as hybrid state:** Lucid dreamers occupy an unusual AIM position — high A, internal I, but partially recovered aminergic M (reflected in gamma-band frontal EEG). This accurately predicts the lucid dreamer's ability to reflect on and remember their state ([[concept-lucid-dreaming|Lucid Dreaming]]).
- **Psychosis analogy:** Acute psychosis maps near REM in AIM space (high A, partially internal I, possibly shifted M), explaining why schizophrenic hallucinations share phenomenological features with REM dreams.
- **Anaesthesia and coma:** States of unconsciousness occupy the low-A corner, regardless of I or M — consistent with global workspace theories of consciousness requiring a minimum activation threshold.

## Recent Developments

- **Neurochemical refinement:** The original aminergic/cholinergic axis has been refined with evidence that the neuromodulatory landscape of sleep is richer — involving noradrenaline, serotonin, dopamine, ACh, histamine, and orexin in overlapping patterns. The AIM model's M axis is a useful simplification.
- **Continuous state monitoring:** High-density EEG and multimodal neuroimaging allow real-time tracking of the A and I dimensions, making AIM an empirically testable framework rather than purely conceptual.
- **Protoconsciousness extension:** In [[source-hobson-2009|Hobson (2009)]], the AIM model is extended to propose that REM sleep is not just a variation of waking but is developmentally and evolutionarily primary — the default state from which waking consciousness is derived.
