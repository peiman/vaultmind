## Overview

A neuron (or nerve cell) is the fundamental signaling unit of the nervous system: an electrically excitable cell that receives, integrates, and transmits information through a combination of electrical and chemical signals. Neurons are organized into networks whose patterned activity gives rise to perception, motor control, learning, and cognition. The human brain contains on the order of 86 billion neurons, each making thousands of synaptic contacts.

What distinguishes a neuron from other cells is the polarized morphology that supports directional signal flow — input on one side, output on the other — and an excitable membrane that can generate the [[action-potential|Action Potential]] as a regenerating, all-or-none spike.

## Key Properties

- **Soma (cell body):** Houses the nucleus and most protein synthesis machinery. Acts as the integration zone where dendritic inputs sum.
- **Dendrites:** Branching cellular extensions that form the primary input surface. Synapses from upstream neurons land on dendritic spines and shafts; the resulting postsynaptic potentials propagate passively toward the soma.
- **Axon:** A single, long, cable-like projection that carries the action potential away from the soma. Its initial segment (axon hillock) is the typical spike-initiation zone because of its high density of voltage-gated sodium channels.
- **Axon terminal:** The presynaptic ending where the action potential triggers calcium-dependent neurotransmitter release onto downstream cells.
- **Synapse:** The contact point — usually chemical, occasionally electrical — between an axon terminal and the next cell's dendrite, soma, or axon. See [[synaptic-transmission|Synaptic Transmission]].
- **Functional types:** Sensory neurons (transduce stimuli), motor neurons (drive muscles and glands), and interneurons (mediate within-circuit computation, the dominant class in the CNS).

## Connections

The neuron is the substrate for every other concept in the cellular-neuroscience cluster. Its excitable membrane, governed by [[membrane-potential|Membrane Potential]] and [[ion-channels|Ion Channels]], gives rise to the [[action-potential|Action Potential]]; its presynaptic terminals release [[neurotransmitters|Neurotransmitters]] to drive [[synaptic-transmission|Synaptic Transmission]]; and the long-term modification of synapses between co-active neurons is the cellular form of [[hebbian-learning|Hebbian Learning]].
