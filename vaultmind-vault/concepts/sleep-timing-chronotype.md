## Overview

**Chronotype** is an individual's innate preference for the timing of sleep and wakefulness — from extreme "morning larks" to extreme "evening owls" — determined primarily by the intrinsic period of the circadian clock and its light-sensitivity. Chronotype is not a preference or a lifestyle choice; it has a heritability of approximately 50%, shifts predictably across the lifespan (childhood → peak eveningness in late adolescence → gradual return to morningness with age), and is stable within individuals across years.

**Social jetlag** (Wittmann, Dinich, Merrow & Roenneberg, 2006) is the discrepancy in hours between mid-sleep time on work/school days versus free days — a measure of how much the social schedule forces individuals to sleep at biologically suboptimal times. It is functionally equivalent to weekly transmeridian travel: the body clock is shifted east by Monday morning and west again by the weekend.

## How It Works

The circadian clock (SCN-driven) gates sleep opportunity by controlling the timing of the melatonin rise (dim-light melatonin onset, DLMO), which signals the biological evening. Late chronotypes have a later DLMO — typically 11 PM to 1 AM rather than 9–10 PM — meaning their biological night begins when many social schedules require them to be asleep. Forcing sleep before DLMO produces sleep that is less restorative and contains less SWS; waking before biological waking time truncates the REM-rich final sleep cycles.

Adolescence uniquely compounds this: the adolescent phase delay (Hagenauer et al., 2009) is driven by two independent biological mechanisms — slower homeostatic sleep pressure buildup and a later-running circadian clock — both of which push preferred sleep timing later by 2–3 hours relative to childhood.

## Key Findings

- **Social jetlag prevalence**: In the Munich Chronotype Questionnaire (MCTQ) sample of ~65,000 Europeans, the average social jetlag was ~1 hour; late chronotypes experienced 2–3 hours (Wittmann et al., 2006).
- **Health correlates**: Social jetlag correlates with higher BMI, increased tobacco and alcohol use, and greater rates of depression and metabolic syndrome, even after controlling for total sleep duration.
- **Cognitive performance**: Performance on attention and memory tasks is worse when forced to perform at circadian-misaligned times, even after controlling for total sleep (Hasher, Goldstein & Zacks, 1991; Schmidt et al., 2007).
- **The adolescent problem**: Most adolescents have a biological DLMO around midnight; 7–8 AM school start times require waking during what the brain perceives as the middle of the night. Curcio et al. (2006) and Hagenauer et al. (2009) both document the consequences.
- **Daylight saving time "natural experiment"**: DST transitions produce measurable spikes in traffic accidents, cardiac events, and decreased performance on standardised tests — demonstrating acute effects of even one hour of circadian misalignment.

## Practical Implications

1. **Identify your chronotype honestly**: Mid-sleep time on free, non-alarm days is the most reliable self-assessment. If it is 4 AM (MSFT = 4 AM), you are an extreme evening type.
2. **Reduce social jetlag where possible**: Negotiate later start times with employers, schedule cognitively demanding work for your biological peak (typically ~2 hours after natural waking), avoid extremely early commitments.
3. **Light therapy for phase advance**: Morning bright light exposure (~10,000 lux for 30 minutes) can advance the circadian clock by 0.5–1 hour per day for extreme evening types who need to shift earlier.
4. **Evening light restriction**: Blue-light blocking after 9 PM suppresses melatonin secretion and delays sleep onset in late chronotypes. The evidence for blue-blocking glasses is moderate; simply dimming screens and room lighting is well-supported.
5. **Do not attempt to "push through"**: A late chronotype forced onto an early schedule does not adapt biologically in the way that jet travellers eventually adapt. The mismatch persists.
