## Overview

Sleep is not a single homogeneous state but an organised sequence of discrete stages, distinguished by characteristic electroencephalographic (EEG), electromyographic (EMG), and electro-oculographic (EOG) signatures. The standard taxonomy divides sleep into **non-rapid eye movement** (NREM) sleep — subdivided into N1, N2, and N3 — and **rapid eye movement** (REM) sleep. This classification, formalised by Rechtschaffen and Kales (1968) and later revised by the American Academy of Sleep Medicine (2007), provides the universal scaffold for sleep research, clinical diagnosis, and mechanistic studies of memory consolidation.

Across a normal 7–9 hour night, sleep organises into 4–6 **ultradian cycles** of roughly 90 minutes each. Early cycles are dominated by deep NREM (N3); later cycles shift toward REM. This gradient has profound consequences for memory: declarative consolidation depends preferentially on early-night slow-wave sleep, while procedural and emotional memory shows benefit from late-night REM-rich cycles.

## How It Works

**N1 (light NREM):** The transition from wakefulness, lasting 1–5 minutes. Alpha rhythm (8–12 Hz) gives way to theta (4–7 Hz). Hypnic jerks are common. Consciousness is reduced but easily roused. Not associated with robust memory processing.

**N2 (intermediate NREM):** Constitutes ~50% of total sleep time. Defined by the appearance of **sleep spindles** (11–16 Hz, 0.5–3 s bursts) and **K-complexes** (large, slow EEG deflections). Both are generated by thalamocortical circuits. Spindles are increasingly recognised as critical for declarative memory consolidation (see [[concept-sleep-spindles|Sleep Spindles]]).

**N3 (slow-wave sleep, SWS):** Defined by >20% of an epoch containing high-amplitude (>75 µV) slow waves (0.5–2 Hz). Arousal threshold is highest here. Growth hormone is secreted. [[concept-sharp-wave-ripples|Sharp-wave ripples]] from the hippocampus are nested within slow oscillations during this stage (see [[concept-hippocampal-cortical-dialogue|Hippocampal-Cortical Dialogue]]).

**REM sleep:** EEG resembles waking (low-amplitude, high-frequency desynchronised activity). Defined by rapid conjugate eye movements, near-complete skeletal muscle atonia, and vivid dreaming. Hippocampal theta oscillations are prominent. ACh is high; noradrenaline and serotonin are near-absent. See [[concept-rem-sleep-physiology|REM Sleep Physiology]] for mechanistic detail.

## Key Findings

- **Polysomnography (PSG):** The gold standard for sleep staging combines EEG (typically 6+ channels), chin EMG, and bilateral EOG. Portable devices (actigraphy, consumer wearables) are validated for tracking sleep duration but have limited stage discrimination.
- **Stage-specific memory benefits:** [[source-plihal-born-1997|Plihal & Born (1997)]] showed that early-night sleep (SWS-rich) preferentially benefits declarative memory, while late-night sleep (REM-rich) benefits procedural memory — the first clean dissociation of stage functions.
- **Glymphatic clearance:** N3 is the primary clearance state for the [[concept-glymphatic-system|glymphatic system]]; the interstitial space expands most during slow-wave sleep.
- **Ageing and N3:** Slow-wave sleep declines sharply with age — from ~20% of total sleep in young adults to <5% in the elderly — which may contribute to age-related memory deficits and increased Alzheimer's risk.
- **Sleep stage rebound:** Selective REM deprivation causes "REM rebound" on recovery nights; N3 rebound (elevated SWA) occurs after total sleep deprivation, suggesting homeostatic regulation of each stage separately.

## Recent Developments

- **AASM 2007 revision:** The original 4-stage Rechtschaffen–Kales system merged stages 3 and 4 into a single N3, reflecting their common functional profile.
- **Micro-architecture:** Beyond gross staging, researchers now quantify spindle density, slow oscillation coupling, and ripple rates as finer-grained indices of consolidation quality.
- **Sleep EEG as a biomarker:** Reduced spindle density and SWA are early markers in Alzheimer's disease (Mander et al. 2017), linking sleep-stage disruption causally to memory pathology.
- **Stage-specific stimulation:** Closed-loop systems now deliver auditory or transcranial stimulation phase-locked to slow oscillations during N3, boosting slow-wave amplitude and associated memory gains ([[source-ngo-2013|Ngo et al. 2013, Neuron]]).

## Connections

Sleep stages are the temporal scaffold within which all sleep-dependent memory mechanisms operate. [[concept-slow-wave-activity|Slow-Wave Activity]] and [[concept-sleep-spindles|Sleep Spindles]] are the defining signals of N3 and N2 respectively. The [[concept-hippocampal-cortical-dialogue|hippocampal-cortical dialogue]] is gated to NREM stages. [[concept-rem-sleep-physiology|REM sleep]] provides a distinct consolidation environment biased toward emotional and procedural traces. Sleep stage disruption — whether by deprivation, fragmentation, or pathology — degrades consolidation in ways that mirror what is known about each stage's mechanistic contributions.

For VaultMind, the ultradian architecture of sleep is the biological precedent for staged processing pipelines: coarse deduplication and structural consolidation occur first (analogous to early-night N3), while associative enrichment and cross-domain linking happen in later passes (analogous to late-night REM).
