## Overview

**Targeted Memory Reactivation (TMR)** is a non-invasive technique for selectively enhancing the consolidation of specific memories by presenting learning-associated cues — sounds, odors, or words — during sleep, typically during slow-wave sleep (SWS). The logic is simple: if memory consolidation during sleep depends on hippocampal replay of waking experiences, then re-presenting the sensory context of that experience should trigger further replay and strengthen consolidation. More than a decade of studies has validated this prediction: TMR reliably improves memory for cued material relative to matched uncued controls.

## How It Works

During slow-wave sleep, the hippocampus spontaneously replays sequences associated with recent experience, coordinated with cortical slow oscillations and sleep spindles — the oscillatory scaffold for hippocampal-cortical memory transfer (see [[sharp-wave-ripples|Sharp-Wave Ripples]]). Presenting a cue that was present during learning re-activates the associated hippocampal representation, driving it into another round of consolidation. The effect is believed to work through the same machinery as spontaneous replay, not through arousal: TMR cues that wake the participant do not improve memory.

Three sensory modalities have been validated:
- **Odors** (Rasch et al., 2007): Rose odor present during encoding; re-presented during SWS. Spatial memory improved; no effect during REM or waking.
- **Sounds** (Rudoy et al., 2009): Object-characteristic sounds softly replayed during SWS. Within-subject, item-level enhancement — cued items better remembered than uncued from the same list.
- **Verbal cues** (Schreiner & Rasch, 2015): Foreign-language words replayed during SWS. Vocabulary retention enhanced; effect correlated with sleep spindle activity during cue delivery.

## Key Findings

- **SWS specificity**: TMR effects consistently require slow-wave sleep; the same cues during REM or waking produce no reliable benefit.
- **Item-level precision**: The Rudoy et al. within-subject design shows selective strengthening of cued vs. uncued items — TMR does not simply improve global sleep quality.
- **Spindle coupling**: The Schreiner & Rasch data suggest cues are most effective when delivered during spindle-rich epochs within SWS, implicating the SO–spindle–ripple coupling that mediates hippocampal-cortical transfer.
- **Dose and intensity**: Cues must be sub-arousal (≤50–60 dB in most studies); louder cues that cause EEG microarousals do not improve memory and may impair it (Schreiner et al., 2015, Nature Communications).
- **Procedural memory**: TMR effects are strongest for declarative memory (spatial, semantic, verbal). Evidence for procedural motor learning is weaker and inconsistent.
- **Emotional memory**: TMR with fear-conditioned stimuli can also influence emotional memory, with potential therapeutic applications for trauma.

## Recent Developments

- **At-home TMR devices**: Consumer and research-grade wearables (e.g., SoundMind, the Dreem headband) now implement simplified TMR protocols. Closed-loop versions that detect SWS before playing cues are more effective than fixed-schedule versions.
- **Schema congruence**: TMR is more effective for memories that fit into an existing schema (Tamminen et al.); isolated arbitrary associations show smaller or less reliable effects.
- **Language learning applications**: Multiple labs have replicated foreign-language TMR with spoken words and translated words, including in classroom-adjacent settings.
- **Mechanistic fMRI/EEG**: Studies confirm that TMR cues produce detectable hippocampal activation during sleep, and that the magnitude of this activation predicts next-morning recall.

## Practical Implications

1. **Pair learning with distinctive sensory cues**: A specific scent, ambient sound, or background music that is unique to a study session can serve as a TMR cue. Replay it softly during subsequent sleep.
2. **Smartphone TMR apps**: Apps that replay audio cues after a delay long enough for SWS onset (~30–45 minutes after sleep) are emerging. The evidence base for at-home TMR is still developing but early results are promising.
3. **Study-sleep timing**: TMR only works during the first consolidation window after learning — the subsequent night's sleep is most effective. The further from learning, the weaker the effect.
4. **Do not use alarm sounds or anything likely to cause arousal**: Sub-threshold volume is critical. The goal is undetected (to the sleeper) replay, not conscious reminder.
5. **Best for declarative material**: Spatial layouts, vocabulary, factual associations. Do not expect robust TMR for motor or procedural skills.

## Connections

TMR exploits the same [[neural-replay|Neural Replay]] machinery that the brain uses spontaneously during [[sharp-wave-ripples|Sharp-Wave Ripples]]. It is a targeted version of the spontaneous consolidation process, not a separate mechanism. The [[closed-loop-sleep-stimulation|Closed-Loop Sleep Stimulation]] approach (Ngo et al., 2013; Marshall et al., 2006) complements TMR by enhancing the slow oscillation infrastructure that supports replay, rather than directly triggering specific memories.
