## Overview

Sensory neurons (afferent neurons) convert physical and chemical events in the body and environment into electrical signals that the central nervous system can read. The conversion process is *sensory transduction*: a stimulus opens or closes ion channels in the receptor terminal, depolarizing or hyperpolarizing the membrane; if the receptor potential reaches threshold, action potentials propagate centrally.

Most somatosensory neurons have their cell bodies in dorsal root ganglia (for body) or cranial nerve ganglia (for face and viscera) and a *pseudounipolar* morphology — a single axon emerging from the soma that bifurcates into a peripheral branch (going to the receptor) and a central branch (entering the spinal cord or brainstem). Special senses (vision, audition, taste, vestibular) use bipolar neurons or specialized receptor cells that synapse onto sensory neurons.

## Anatomy & Function

**Receptor types by stimulus modality**
- **Mechanoreceptors:** touch, pressure, vibration, stretch, proprioception. Includes Meissner, Pacinian, Merkel, Ruffini corpuscles in skin; muscle spindles and Golgi tendon organs in muscle; hair cells in cochlea and vestibular organs.
- **Thermoreceptors:** warm and cold receptors using TRP-family ion channels (TRPV1 for noxious heat / capsaicin; TRPM8 for cool / menthol).
- **Photoreceptors:** rods (scotopic) and cones (photopic, color) in the retina; phototransduction via rhodopsin/opsins, transducin, and cGMP-gated channels.
- **Chemoreceptors:** olfactory receptor neurons (G-protein-coupled receptors), taste receptor cells, glomus cells sensing blood O2/CO2/pH.
- **Nociceptors:** specialized free nerve endings detecting tissue-damaging stimuli; include A-delta (sharp, fast) and C-fiber (dull, slow) populations.

**Pseudounipolar morphology**
The peripheral and central branches share continuity; action potentials initiated at the receptor terminal propagate past the soma directly to the central terminal in the spinal dorsal horn or brainstem. This arrangement minimizes synaptic delay between transduction and CNS entry.

**Adaptation**
- *Tonic* (slowly adapting) receptors signal sustained stimuli (e.g., Merkel cells, muscle spindles).
- *Phasic* (rapidly adapting) receptors signal change and onset/offset (e.g., Pacinian corpuscles).

## Connections

Sensory neurons are the afferent counterpart to efferent [[motor-neurons|Motor Neurons]]; their convergence on the spinal cord, modulated by [[interneurons|Interneurons]], implements reflex arcs (e.g., the monosynaptic stretch reflex in which Ia spindle afferents directly excite alpha motor neurons). The [[action-potential|Action Potential]] generated at the sensory terminal is the universal currency they use to report stimulus intensity (rate code) and timing. Sensory transduction motifs reappear throughout neuroscience: even non-sensory neurons use TRP channels and GPCR cascades to detect intracellular and intercellular signals.
