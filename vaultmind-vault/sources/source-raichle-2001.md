# Raichle et al. — A Default Mode of Brain Function (2001)

Raichle and colleagues, looking at PET data across many cognitive tasks, noticed a strange pattern: a specific set of brain regions — posterior cingulate / precuneus, medial prefrontal cortex, lateral parietal cortex / angular gyrus, and parts of medial temporal lobe — consistently *decreased* their activity below resting baseline whenever subjects engaged in goal-directed tasks. The "baseline" itself was active. Resting brain was not idle brain.

They proposed that this network represents a *default mode* of brain function — an organised, ongoing intrinsic activity that is suspended when attention turns outward to a task, and resumes when it turns inward. Subsequent work tied the Default Mode Network (DMN) to mind-wandering, autobiographical memory, simulation of past and future events, theory of mind, and self-referential thought. DMN dysfunction has since been implicated in Alzheimer's, depression, schizophrenia, and autism.

The finding inverted a long-standing assumption that "rest" was the baseline against which "activation" should be measured. Raichle's later work made this even sharper: most of the brain's energy budget is spent on intrinsic activity, not stimulus-driven activity.

For VaultMind, the DMN motivates an asymmetry in how an agent uses idle vs. active time. Idle / between-query intervals are not dead time — they are when offline integration, simulation, and consolidation should happen.
