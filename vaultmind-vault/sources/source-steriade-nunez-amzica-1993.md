# Steriade, Nuñez & Amzica — A Novel Slow (<1 Hz) Oscillation of Neocortical Neurons in vivo (1993)

This paper provided the first systematic intracellular characterisation of the slow oscillation in vivo, recording neocortical neurons in anaesthetised cats. Steriade's group described the alternating UP states (sustained depolarisation, ~0.5–1 s) and DOWN states (hyperpolarisation/silence, ~0.3–0.5 s) that constitute the cellular basis of what the EEG records as slow waves. Critically, they demonstrated that isolated cortical slabs — disconnected from thalamus and brainstem — still generate this rhythm, establishing its origin as intrinsically cortical.

The companion paper (Steriade et al. 1993b, *J. Neurosci.* 13:3266–3283) extended this by showing the temporal relationship between the slow oscillation and the faster sleep rhythms (spindles, delta), demonstrating that spindles are grouped by the slow oscillation — the first direct evidence for what would become the triple-coupling hierarchy.
