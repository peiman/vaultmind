# Mazza et al. — Relearn Faster and Retain Longer (2016)

Compared two spaced-learning schedules across six months in university students learning Swahili–French word pairs: one group studied on two consecutive days (Session 1 → Session 2 same week) then had a long gap before test; another group studied once, slept, then had the second session span a sleep boundary. The sleep-intersecting schedule produced dramatically better six-month retention. Moreover, relearning during the second session was faster — participants reached criterion in fewer trials — precisely because sleep had consolidated the first session's learning, making relearning more efficient.

The key applied finding: **not just when you sleep, but whether sleep falls between practice sessions**. The spacing effect and sleep interact multiplicatively. A study-sleep-review schedule is far superior to study-study-sleep, even with identical total study time. This is perhaps the single most actionable finding for student study schedules.
