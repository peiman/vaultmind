# Curcio, Ferrara & De Gennaro — Sleep Loss, Learning Capacity and Academic Performance (2006)

A comprehensive narrative review synthesising experimental and epidemiological evidence on the relationship between sleep loss and academic/cognitive performance in children and adolescents. Key findings: (1) even partial sleep restriction (reducing from 8–9 to 6–7 hours) degrades attention, working memory, and declarative learning; (2) subjective sleepiness does not accurately track objective cognitive impairment — people feel adapted while continuing to perform poorly; (3) academic grades correlate negatively with delayed sleep timing and shorter sleep duration across multiple large samples; (4) the adolescent circadian shift toward eveningness is a biological reality, not a social preference, making early school start times a structural mismatch.

The review is frequently cited in policy discussions around school start times and provides a solid empirical anchor for the claim that chronic partial sleep restriction — common in students — has measurable academic consequences.
