# MacDonald, Lepage, Eden & Eichenbaum — Hippocampal Time Cells (2011)

The Eichenbaum lab trained rats on a delayed odor-association task: smell odor A, wait 10 seconds in a fixed location, then choose. Across the empty delay period, where the rat was stationary and external sensory input was nearly constant, ensembles of CA1 pyramidal cells fired in stereotyped sequences — each cell tiling a different moment of the delay. They called these "time cells", in deliberate parallel to place cells.

Time cells were not just clocks. When the delay length was changed, the cells *retimed*: the population rescaled or remapped to fill the new interval, much as place cells remap when the environment changes. This established that the hippocampus codes an experience along temporal as well as spatial axes — and that the same circuit can produce both, depending on what dimension of experience is task-relevant.

The result is foundational for episodic memory: episodes are events located in *both* place and time, and the hippocampus appears to provide the scaffolding for both coordinates.

For VaultMind, time cells motivate temporal-decay structures in episode/event storage: episodes have a "when" coordinate, not just a "what" coordinate, and recency-weighted retrieval mirrors the natural rescaling of time-cell sequences.
