# Mednick et al. — Sleep-Dependent Learning: A Nap Is as Good as a Night (2003)

Perceptual learning on a texture discrimination task (TDT) shows a well-established deterioration across a day of waking: morning performance is better than afternoon performance. Mednick et al. showed that a 60–90 minute afternoon nap containing both SWS and REM sleep fully reversed this deterioration, restoring performance to morning levels. Critically, a 60–90 minute nap was "as good as a night" — the same restoration that a full night of sleep produced could be achieved in an hour and a half, provided the nap contained the right sleep stages.

Naps containing only SWS helped, but naps with both SWS and REM were optimal — suggesting that different memory systems (hippocampal declarative vs. neocortical procedural) may have stage-specific consolidation windows. The study was foundational in establishing naps as a pragmatically actionable intervention: you don't have to wait for overnight sleep to consolidate perceptual learning.
