# McGaugh — Memory: A Century of Consolidation (2000)

McGaugh's review synthesized a century of consolidation research beginning with Müller and Pilzecker's 1900 observation that memories are susceptible to disruption shortly after formation but become progressively resistant over time. He traced the neurobiological mechanisms identified across that century: protein synthesis is required to stabilize synaptic changes within hours of learning; hormonal systems — particularly epinephrine and cortisol released during arousal or stress — modulate consolidation via the amygdala, which projects to hippocampal and cortical memory circuits; and sleep plays a critical role in systems-level consolidation, transferring hippocampally dependent memories toward cortical storage. The review established [[memory-consolidation|Memory Consolidation]] as a time-dependent, biologically regulated process, not simply passive trace decay.

A key practical implication of consolidation research is that emotional arousal — via amygdala-mediated hormonal modulation — selectively strengthens consolidation of events deemed significant. This explains why emotionally salient experiences are remembered with unusual vividness and durability. McGaugh's century-long survey also revealed the limits of the [[forgetting-curve|Forgetting Curve]]: Ebbinghaus's neutral nonsense syllables underestimate retention for meaningful, emotionally tagged material, because consolidation is not uniform across memory types. The review has been cited thousands of times and remains the standard entry point for anyone approaching consolidation neuroscience.
