# Sorrells et al. — Human Hippocampal Neurogenesis Drops to Undetectable Levels in Adults (2018)

Sorrells and colleagues in the Alvarez-Buylla lab examined 59 human hippocampi spanning fetal life to age 77, using stains for proliferation (Ki-67), neural progenitors (Sox2, GFAP-d), and immature neurons (DCX, PSA-NCAM). Counts of proliferating progenitors and DCX+ immature neurons in the dentate gyrus dropped sharply through childhood. By age 13 they were rare. In adult tissue (18-77 y) the authors report finding *no* DCX+ immature neurons.

This contradicted the dominant view, established from earlier BrdU and ¹⁴C-dating studies (Eriksson 1998; Spalding 2013), that the human dentate gyrus continuously generates new neurons throughout life — a view central to many theories linking neurogenesis to mood, learning, and Alzheimer's risk.

The paper triggered a hard, ongoing methodological dispute. Companion and follow-up papers ([[source-boldrini-2018|Boldrini et al. 2018]]; Moreno-Jiménez et al. 2019) using different fixation protocols and antibodies report that immature neurons *are* detectable into the eighth decade. The disagreement now appears to hinge on tissue handling, post-mortem interval, and antibody specificity. As of 2026 the field's consensus is best summarised as: adult human hippocampal neurogenesis, if it occurs, occurs at a much lower rate than in rodents and is fragile to detect.

For VaultMind the take-away is methodological: replication failures are often substrate-handling failures, not theory failures. The same argument applies to flaky retrieval evals.
