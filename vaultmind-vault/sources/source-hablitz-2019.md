# Hablitz et al. — Increased Glymphatic Influx Is Correlated with High EEG Delta Power and Low Heart Rate (2019)

This Science Advances paper directly quantified the relationship between brain oscillatory state (EEG delta power, a proxy for slow-wave activity) and glymphatic fluid influx, using simultaneous two-photon imaging of fluorescent tracers in the paravascular space of mice under anaesthesia. The key finding: EEG delta power (0.5–4 Hz, analogous to SWA during natural sleep) was the strongest predictor of glymphatic influx rate among the physiological parameters measured, including heart rate, respiratory rate, and blood pressure.

The paper established two related points. First, the coupling between delta oscillations and glymphatic influx is not mediated purely by heart rate (which is lower during high-delta states) — both variables independently predict glymphatic influx. Second, the relationship held across different anaesthetic regimes that differentially modulate delta power, suggesting a direct electrophysiological coupling rather than a shared downstream cause.

This work mechanistically linked the two major functions of slow-wave sleep — memory consolidation (via hippocampal replay nested in slow oscillations) and waste clearance (via glymphatic influx) — to the same EEG signal: delta power. The same brain state that maximises consolidation also maximises clearance. This co-regulation likely reflects a shared upstream mechanism: suppression of noradrenaline release from the locus coeruleus, which simultaneously promotes slow oscillations and relaxes astrocytic endfeet (increasing paravascular space).

For VaultMind, Hablitz et al. confirm that consolidation and maintenance are co-scheduled, not competing, operations — both are maximally efficient in the same brain state, reinforcing the case for a unified offline maintenance window.
