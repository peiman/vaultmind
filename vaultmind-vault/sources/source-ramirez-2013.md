# Ramirez et al. — Creating a False Memory in the Hippocampus (2013)

Ramirez, Liu, Lin, Suh, Pignatelli, Redondo, Ryan, and Tonegawa took the engram-tagging method from [[source-liu-2012|Liu et al. 2012]] and reversed the script. Mice explored Context A while dentate gyrus cells were tagged with channelrhodopsin. The next day, in Context B, the tagged Context-A engram was optically reactivated at the same time the mice received foot shocks. When the mice were later returned to Context A — where they had never been shocked — they froze.

This is a memory the brain never actually formed in the natural way. The animal's hippocampus was tricked into binding a fear it acquired in B onto a representation of A. The result establishes that engrams are not just read-outs of past experience: they are addressable substrates that can be re-bound to new associations. It also operationalises a long-standing finding from Loftus and others that human episodic memory is reconstructive and editable — false memories are not a glitch, they are what you get when reconsolidation links the wrong representations.

For VaultMind this is a cautionary signal about reconsolidation as edit window: every time a note is loaded, reasoned about, and re-saved, it is being re-bound to whatever context the agent was in. That is power and risk simultaneously.
