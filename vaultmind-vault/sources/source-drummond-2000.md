# Drummond et al. — Altered Brain Response to Verbal Learning Following Sleep Deprivation (2000)

The first fMRI study to examine how sleep deprivation changes neural encoding. After 35 hours of total sleep deprivation, participants attempted to encode word pairs. Behavioural recall was impaired. Neuroimaging showed reduced prefrontal and parietal activation during encoding — regions essential for semantic elaboration and strategic encoding. Strikingly, the temporal lobe (a simpler, associative encoding route) showed compensatory increased activation, suggesting the sleep-deprived brain attempts to substitute a cruder encoding strategy.

This provides a neural mechanism for why sleep-deprived learning is not just slower, but qualitatively different: the semantic depth-of-processing that makes memories stick is selectively impaired, while surface association encoding partially compensates. The implication is that sleep deprivation does not simply reduce capacity — it degrades encoding quality.
