# Marshall et al. — Boosting Slow Oscillations During Sleep Potentiates Memory (2006)

During early NREM sleep, participants received transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS) at 0.75 Hz — matching the natural frequency of cortical slow oscillations. This oscillatory tDCS significantly increased the amplitude of endogenous slow oscillations and, crucially, the associated fast sleep spindle activity. Next-morning declarative memory (word pairs) was significantly enhanced; procedural memory (motor sequence) was not, confirming the declarative specificity of slow-wave-dependent consolidation.

This was the first demonstration that non-invasive brain stimulation during sleep can causally enhance memory — and that the mechanism operates through slow oscillation amplification, not arousal. The combination of tDCS + sleep gives the effect a translational plausibility: the stimulation parameters are safe, non-invasive, and deliverable without waking the participant.
