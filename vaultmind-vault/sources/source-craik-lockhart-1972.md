# Craik & Lockhart — Levels of Processing (1972)

Craik and Lockhart challenged the then-dominant multi-store model of memory — in which retention depends on residence time in a short-term buffer — by proposing that memory durability is a byproduct of processing depth during encoding, not of how long information sits in storage. They described a continuum from shallow (structural or phonemic analysis) to deep (semantic elaboration), arguing that processing an item's meaning automatically produces a richer, more distinctive memory trace than processing its surface features. Experiments showed that orienting tasks directing subjects toward meaning produced dramatically better recognition than tasks directing them toward sound or appearance, with no difference in initial exposure time.

The levels-of-processing framework reframed encoding as an active, meaning-making process rather than passive registration. The key implication — that elaborative encoding (connecting new material to existing knowledge, generating examples, explaining implications) yields far superior long-term retention — has been confirmed across hundreds of studies and forms the empirical backbone of techniques like elaborative interrogation, self-explanation, and the generation effect. Though later criticized for circular definition (depth was sometimes inferred from retention rather than independently measured), the framework's core insight about semantic elaboration remains unchallenged.
