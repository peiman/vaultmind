# Buzsáki — Hippocampal Sharp Wave-Ripple: A Cognitive Biomarker (2015)

This long review synthesises three decades of Buzsáki-lab work on the sharp-wave ripple (SPW-R), the most synchronous population event in the mammalian brain. SPW-Rs originate in the recurrent CA3 network: a sharp wave of depolarisation in CA3 drives a fast (140-250 Hz) ripple oscillation in CA1, packing thousands of neurons into a 50-150 ms burst that propagates to neocortex, basal ganglia, and other targets.

Buzsáki argues SPW-Rs are not a passive after-effect of waking activity but the offline computational core of memory: they replay temporally compressed sequences from waking, both forward and reverse, drawing on recent and remote experience to support consolidation, planning, and even creative recombination. SPW-Rs occur during quiet wakefulness and non-REM sleep — the brain's "off-line" regime — and their disruption impairs memory in rodents. Their pathological forms ("p-ripples") mark epileptogenic tissue and appear in models of schizophrenia and Alzheimer's disease.

The review elevates SPW-Rs from an electrophysiological curiosity to a candidate "cognitive biomarker" — a measurable physiological event whose properties track memory function and dysfunction.

For VaultMind the SPW-R framing is a reminder that consolidation is event-driven, not continuous: discrete bursts move information from labile to stable storage. Periodic batch jobs, not continuous streams, are the right engineering shape.
