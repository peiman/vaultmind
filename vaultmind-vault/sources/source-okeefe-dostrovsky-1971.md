# O'Keefe & Dostrovsky — The Hippocampus as a Spatial Map (1971)

While recording extracellularly from CA1 of freely moving rats, O'Keefe and Dostrovsky noticed that some hippocampal pyramidal cells fired only when the rat's head was in a specific small region of the open arena, and were nearly silent everywhere else. They called these "place units" and proposed that the rat hippocampus contains a "cognitive map" — a graded neural representation of locations in space, distinct from any sensory cue.

The result was greeted with skepticism (the hippocampus was at the time mostly associated with memory after H.M., not space) but reproductions across labs forced the field to accept that the hippocampus implements an allocentric spatial code. The concept of a [[place-cells|Place Cell]] became the anchor for thirty years of spatial neuroscience and led directly to O'Keefe's share of the 2014 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine.

For VaultMind the place-cell finding is a model of what content-addressable retrieval looks like at the neural level: a sparse population code where the active subset *is* the content. Wikilink graphs and embedding neighbourhoods are the digital echo of this — proximity in graph or vector space stands in for proximity in cognitive space.
