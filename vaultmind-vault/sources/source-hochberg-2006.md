## Hochberg et al. — BrainGate First Human Trial (2006)

The Nature paper reporting the first BrainGate pilot: Matthew Nagle, a man with C3-C4 tetraplegia, was implanted with a 96-channel [[utah-array|Utah array]] in motor cortex and used the recorded population activity to control a computer cursor, open email, operate a TV, draw on the screen, and command a prosthetic hand and a robotic arm — three years after his spinal cord injury. The study showed that motor cortex remains task-modulated long after deafferentation and that intracortical signals can be decoded into useful control in a real human user. This was the foundational clinical demonstration that motivated the modern intracortical BCI field, including subsequent BrainGate and BrainGate2 trials and Neuralink's later human work.

**Citation:** Hochberg, L.R., Serruya, M.D., Friehs, G.M., Mukand, J.A., Saleh, M., Caplan, A.H., Branner, A., Chen, D., Penn, R.D., & Donoghue, J.P. (2006). Neuronal ensemble control of prosthetic devices by a human with tetraplegia. *Nature*, 442(7099), 164-171. DOI: 10.1038/nature04970
