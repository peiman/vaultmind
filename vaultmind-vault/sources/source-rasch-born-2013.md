# Rasch & Born — About Sleep's Role in Memory (2013)

This is the most comprehensive review of sleep and memory ever published — 86 pages in *Physiological Reviews*, synthesising over 1,000 citations. Rasch and Born cover the full mechanistic and behavioural landscape: sleep stage contributions to different memory types (declarative, procedural, emotional), the cellular and circuit mechanisms (slow oscillations, spindles, SWRs, theta), targeted memory reactivation experiments, the interplay with emotional memory and prefrontal function, and the SHY vs. ASC theoretical debate.

The review is notable for its rigorous treatment of targeted memory reactivation (TMR) — the experimental paradigm where learning-associated cues are re-presented during specific sleep stages to selectively boost consolidation of the paired material. TMR experiments provide the cleanest causal evidence that the hippocampal-cortical channel can be opened selectively during NREM sleep, boosting ripple-mediated transfer of specific memories. Rasch and Born present this as the linchpin evidence for ASC over a passive interference-reduction account.

The review also provides the most balanced treatment of SHY vs. ASC, arguing for a "complementary" synthesis: SHY describes the background renormalisation that occurs during slow-wave sleep (global downscaling of non-tagged synapses), while ASC describes the selective replay-driven potentiation of tagged, important memories. Both processes co-occur during the same NREM episode, operating on different synaptic populations.

For VaultMind, this review serves as the master reference for the neurobiological justification of offline consolidation design, and the TMR paradigm suggests a concrete feature: the vault could re-expose the agent to recently-learned concepts during low-activity windows via a cued-recall probe, selectively strengthening links to important material.
