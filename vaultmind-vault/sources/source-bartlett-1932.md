# Bartlett — Remembering (1932)

Bartlett's central argument — established through his famous "War of the Ghosts" serial reproduction experiments — was that memory is reconstructive rather than reproductive. When British participants repeatedly recalled an unfamiliar Native American folk story, they did not produce degraded copies; they systematically transformed the story toward familiar cultural schemas, rationalizing strange elements, omitting details that lacked coherent structure, and importing narrative conventions from their own culture. Bartlett concluded that recall is not retrieval of a fixed trace but an active reconstruction shaped by pre-existing knowledge structures he called schemas.

The concept of the [[schema-theory|schema]] — an organized mental framework encoding typical patterns, scripts, and expectations about a domain — became one of the most influential ideas in cognitive science. Bartlett's work predated and anticipated connectionist memory models, script theory (Schank & Abelson, 1977), and frame-based knowledge representation in AI. His core finding — that prior knowledge guides encoding and distorts retrieval toward expected patterns — explains a wide range of phenomena from eyewitness error to expert advantage to the difficulty of learning material that contradicts existing beliefs.

VaultMind treats the user's note graph as an externalized schema system. The structure of [[semantic-networks|wikilinks]], [[MOC|MOCs]], and tag hierarchies reflects the user's conceptual schemas, and VaultMind's retrieval engine weights results by their fit to those schemas — analogous to the schema-congruent advantage Bartlett documented in recall. Crucially, VaultMind also surfaces schema-incongruent notes (isolated notes, broken links, conceptual orphans) as candidates for integration, counteracting the natural tendency toward schema-conforming reconstruction that Bartlett identified as a source of systematic memory distortion.
