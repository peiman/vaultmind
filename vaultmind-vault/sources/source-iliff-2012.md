# Iliff et al. — A Paravascular Pathway Facilitates CSF Flow and Clearance (2012)

Using two-photon imaging in living mice, Iliff and colleagues in Maiken Nedergaard's lab traced fluorescent tracers introduced into the cisterna magna and watched the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) move *into* the brain parenchyma along the outsides of penetrating arteries, mix with interstitial fluid, and drain out along veins. The flow depended on aquaporin-4 (AQP4) water channels on astrocytic endfeet that line the paravascular spaces. AQP4 knockouts showed a 70% reduction in clearance of interstitial amyloid-β.

This was the first description of the "glymphatic" system: a brain-wide, glia-supported, CSF-driven clearance pathway analogous to the lymphatic system the brain was long thought to lack. Aside from its mechanistic novelty, the finding offered a candidate route for the buildup of amyloid-β and tau pathology in Alzheimer's, by linking neurodegeneration to a clearance failure rather than only to overproduction.

For VaultMind the glymphatic finding is one of the cleanest biological cases of "the substrate has to take time off to clean up". Treating maintenance jobs (re-indexing, dedup, link cleanup) as first-class scheduled events, not afterthoughts, has a direct neuroscience analogue.
