# Xie et al. — Sleep Drives Metabolite Clearance from the Adult Brain (2013)

Xie, Kang, and colleagues in the Nedergaard lab measured the brain's interstitial space directly in awake versus sleeping mice, using real-time iontophoresis of tetramethylammonium and two-photon imaging. They found that the interstitial space expands by roughly 60% during natural sleep or anaesthesia, dramatically increasing the rate at which CSF and interstitial fluid mix and at which solutes — including radio-labelled amyloid-β — are cleared from the brain.

This is the mechanistic capstone to [[source-iliff-2012|Iliff et al. 2012]]: the glymphatic system exists, but its throughput is gated by behavioural state. Waking brain is too packed for efficient clearance. Sleeping brain opens up and flushes itself.

The finding gave a new and concrete answer to the question "what is sleep for?" — not only synaptic homeostasis (Tononi) and replay-based consolidation (Wilson, McNaughton, Buzsáki), but also literal waste removal. It also linked chronic sleep disruption causally to neurodegeneration: a brain that never opens up never cleans up.

The work received the 2014 Newcomb Cleveland Prize from AAAS.
