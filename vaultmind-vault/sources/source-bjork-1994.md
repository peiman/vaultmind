# Bjork — Memory and Metamemory Considerations in Training (1994)

This book chapter introduced the term "desirable difficulties" and made the case that training research and educational practice systematically undervalue long-term retention in favor of short-term performance metrics. Bjork reviewed evidence showing that conditions perceived as beneficial during training — massed practice, reduced variability, immediate feedback, re-reading — often produce inferior long-term retention compared to conditions perceived as difficult — spacing, interleaving, delayed feedback, testing.

The metamemory dimension is central: learners and instructors alike are poor judges of what conditions produce durable learning. People feel they are learning more when conditions are easy (high fluency, few errors), but this feeling of learning is often a poor indicator of actual retention. The chapter argues that effective training design must override these metacognitive biases by deliberately building in conditions that feel harder but produce better outcomes.

The chapter appeared in a volume on metacognition edited by Metcalfe and Shimamura, placing desirable difficulties squarely within the literature on knowing about one's own cognition — a connection Bjork has developed further in subsequent work on the distinction between current performance and learning.
