# Friston — The Free-Energy Principle: A Unified Brain Theory? (2010)

Friston argues that any self-organising system at non-equilibrium steady state with its environment must, in effect, minimise an information-theoretic quantity called variational free energy — an upper bound on surprise (the negative log probability of sensory observations under the system's internal model). Brains, on this account, are doing nothing but minimising free energy: by perception (updating the model so it predicts inputs better) or by action (changing inputs so they match predictions).

Within this single principle, Friston subsumes predictive coding, the Bayesian brain, optimal control, neural Darwinism, infomax, and exploration-exploitation under one variational objective. Critics have pushed back that the framework is so general it borders on unfalsifiable, and that key constructs (Markov blankets, generative models the brain "has") are slippery. Defenders argue that the principle yields concrete, testable predictions when paired with specific generative models — most prominently in active inference accounts of perception, action, and interoception.

Whatever one's view on the metaphysics, the 2010 review is the canonical reference for the modern free-energy / active-inference programme.

For VaultMind, the relevant take-away is operational, not metaphysical: agent behaviour can be framed as reducing uncertainty about a model of the world, and retrieval is one tool for doing so. Choosing what to retrieve = choosing what would most reduce expected free energy.
