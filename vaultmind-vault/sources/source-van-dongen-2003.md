# Van Dongen et al. — The Cumulative Cost of Additional Wakefulness (2003)

Controlled laboratory study in which participants were randomised to total sleep deprivation or to sleep restricted to 4, 6, or 8 hours per night for 14 consecutive days. Psychomotor Vigilance Task (PVT) and working memory were measured at regular intervals. Key findings: (1) cognitive deficits from chronic 6-hour sleep (the "I can manage on 6 hours" schedule) accumulated over 14 days to match the impairment of 24 hours of total sleep deprivation; (2) subjective sleepiness stabilised at a subjectively manageable level while objective performance continued to deteriorate — participants did not know how impaired they were; (3) the dose-response was roughly linear below 8 hours.

This is the definitive empirical refutation of the claim that "I function fine on 6 hours." The dissociation between subjective and objective impairment is its most practically important finding: people adapt to feeling sleepy but not to performing poorly.
