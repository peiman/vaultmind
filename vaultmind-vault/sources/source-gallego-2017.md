# Gallego, Perich, Miller & Solla — Neural Manifolds for the Control of Movement (2017)

This influential perspective consolidates a decade of motor-cortex population-recording work (Churchland, Shenoy, Cunningham, Sahani, Kao, and others) into a single framework. Despite tens of thousands of neurons in primary and premotor cortex, their joint activity during reaching reliably lies on a low-dimensional (~10-30 D) "neural manifold" embedded in the much higher-dimensional firing-rate space. The manifold is spanned by a small set of patterns of correlated activity — "neural modes" — whose time-varying activations they argue *are* the relevant control signal for movement.

The framework reframes motor coding from "what does each neuron represent?" to "what are the dominant population dynamics?", and argues that the brain may not represent kinematic variables explicitly at all — instead, it produces movement by traversing trajectories on a manifold whose geometry is shaped by anatomy and learning.

Subsequent work (Gallego 2018, 2020; Safaie 2023) has shown that manifolds are remarkably stable across days and even across animals trained on the same task, and that learning a new task adds dimensions or rotates the manifold rather than tearing it up.

For VaultMind, the manifold idea is a reminder that high-dimensional stores often live on low-dimensional structure, and that the right query is one that aligns with the dominant modes — exactly what good embedding spaces try to do.
