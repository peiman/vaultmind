# Hafting et al. — Microstructure of a Spatial Map in Entorhinal Cortex (2005)

Recording from the dorsocaudal medial entorhinal cortex (dMEC) — one synapse upstream of hippocampal CA1 — the Mosers and colleagues found cells that fired not at one place (like CA1 place cells) but at *many* places, arranged in a regular hexagonal lattice tiling the entire arena. These "grid cells" maintained their lattice geometry across environments, only translating, rotating, or rescaling. Spacing increased systematically from dorsal to ventral dMEC, suggesting a multi-scale spatial code.

Grid cells answered a long-standing question: where does the place-cell code come from? A single grid cell does not specify a location, but a small population of grid cells with different scales and phases uniquely specifies any point in a plane — a kind of biological Fourier basis for space. Hafting et al. is the foundational result that, together with later head-direction, border, and speed cells, defined the entorhinal-hippocampal circuit as the brain's positioning system. May-Britt and Edvard Moser shared the 2014 Nobel Prize with John O'Keefe for this work.

For VaultMind this is suggestive: a multi-scale, periodic basis can encode an entire space with very few cells. Hierarchical or multi-resolution embeddings are a natural analogue.
