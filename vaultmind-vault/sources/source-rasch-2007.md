# Rasch et al. — Odor Cues During Slow-Wave Sleep Prompt Declarative Memory Consolidation (2007)

Participants learned the locations of object-pairs on a grid while a rose odor was present in the room. Half were then re-exposed to the same odor during subsequent slow-wave sleep (SWS); controls received the odor during REM sleep or waking. Those cued during SWS showed significantly better next-morning recall of spatial locations — the first causal demonstration in humans that re-presenting a learning-associated cue during sleep can drive targeted memory consolidation.

The effect was SWS-specific: odor delivery during REM sleep or quiet waking produced no improvement, implicating the hippocampal reactivation machinery that is selectively active during slow-wave sleep. The authors measured hippocampal activity via fMRI and confirmed that hippocampal reactivation was greater in the SWS-cued group, directly linking the behavioural enhancement to a neural mechanism. This paper established **Targeted Memory Reactivation (TMR)** as an experimentally viable paradigm.
