# Yoo, Hu, Gujar, Jolesz & Walker — A Deficit in the Ability to Form New Human Memories Without Sleep (2007)

This paper provided the first fMRI evidence that sleep deprivation impairs new memory encoding at a neural level, not merely at the behavioural level. Yoo and colleagues assigned healthy adults to either a normal sleep group or a total sleep deprivation group, then scanned both groups with fMRI while they encoded a set of 150 emotionally neutral and emotional photographic scenes. Both groups then slept for two nights before receiving a surprise recognition test.

The sleep-deprived group showed a ~40% deficit in subsequent recall of the encoded scenes — but more strikingly, they showed a near-complete loss of hippocampal encoding activity (BOLD response in the hippocampus) relative to the rested group. Medial prefrontal cortex activity, which normally is inversely correlated with successful hippocampal encoding (it gates hippocampal function), was hyperactivated in the sleep-deprived group — suggesting that the prefrontal "gate" was stuck open, flooding the hippocampus with non-specific inhibition and preventing selective encoding.

The paper established that sleep deprivation does not merely slow consolidation after encoding — it fundamentally impairs the formation of new memory traces at the moment of learning. This has important implications for learning schedules, exam preparation, and occupational settings where sleep restriction is routine.

For VaultMind, Yoo et al. establish the encoding-side cost of "running without maintenance": the hippocampal buffer cannot accept new information efficiently when it has not been cleared by offline consolidation. In VaultMind terms: a vault that has not been consolidated recently will show degraded performance on new-note ingestion and link formation, not just on retrieval.
