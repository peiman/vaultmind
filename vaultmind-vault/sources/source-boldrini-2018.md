# Boldrini et al. — Human Hippocampal Neurogenesis Persists Throughout Aging (2018)

Published a month after [[source-sorrells-2018|Sorrells et al. 2018]] and reaching the opposite conclusion, Boldrini and colleagues examined 28 whole post-mortem hippocampi from neurologically and psychiatrically healthy individuals aged 14-79. They report similar numbers of intermediate neural progenitors and thousands of DCX+ / PSA-NCAM+ immature neurons in the dentate gyrus across all ages, alongside a decline in quiescent stem-cell pools, angiogenesis, and dendritic complexity.

The two papers are foundational to a still-unresolved debate about whether adult human neurogenesis is real. Differences trace to tissue source, post-mortem interval, fixation, and antibody choice. The Boldrini group used freshly fixed, intact whole hippocampi from a tissue bank with controlled processing; the Sorrells group used a more heterogeneous sample mix.

Subsequent work has added complications rather than resolved them: Moreno-Jiménez 2019 (Alzheimer's tissue) supports persistence; Franjic 2022 (single-nucleus RNA-seq) sees little molecular evidence for active adult neurogenesis. By 2026 the most defensible position is that adult human hippocampal neurogenesis exists at a low and possibly variable rate, and detecting it is heavily method-dependent.

For VaultMind: when two carefully run experiments produce opposite results, the right move is to identify the moderator (here: tissue handling) rather than to pick a side. Same lesson applies in retrieval evals where benchmark differences swing results.
