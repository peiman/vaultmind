# Wittmann, Dinich, Merrow & Roenneberg — Social Jetlag (2006)

Coined and operationalised the term **social jetlag**: the discrepancy in hours between mid-sleep time on work/school days versus free days. Analysed chronotype data from a large population survey and found that late chronotypes experience the greatest social jetlag — essentially living in a different time zone on weekends versus weekdays. Social jetlag correlated significantly with higher body mass index (BMI) and with tobacco and alcohol use.

The key conceptual contribution is reframing what looks like "laziness" or "irregular habits" as a biologically driven circadian mismatch imposed by social schedules. On average, the European population loses ~1 hour of sleep per night on work days relative to their biological optimum. Social jetlag is a form of chronic partial sleep restriction for late chronotypes — with the cognitive consequences documented by Van Dongen et al. (2003).
