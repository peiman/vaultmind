# Sirota, Csicsvari, Buhl & Buzsáki — Communication Between Neocortex and Hippocampus During Sleep in Rodents (2003)

This PNAS paper by Buzsáki's group provided the first direct evidence that hippocampal sharp-wave ripples (SWRs) are temporally coupled to neocortical slow oscillation UP states during NREM sleep in rodents. Using simultaneous multisite recordings from hippocampus and neocortex in freely behaving rats, Sirota and colleagues showed that SWRs occurred preferentially during the positive (UP) half-cycles of the neocortical slow oscillation — precisely when the cortex was in its maximally excitable state.

This was the key missing link in the consolidation circuit: it had been known that SWRs carry hippocampal replay, and from Steriade's work that cortical slow oscillations gate downstream excitability. Sirota et al. showed that these two facts were connected: hippocampal output is timed to cortical receptivity. The finding established that the hippocampal-cortical transfer is not random noise but a precisely timed dialogue, with the cortex effectively "requesting" hippocampal input during its UP states.

The paper also showed that neocortical firing rates (multi-unit activity) increased immediately after SWR onset — indicating that hippocampal replay output drives cortical responses, not merely correlates with them. This directional evidence (hippocampus → cortex during SWRs) was critical for the two-stage consolidation model.

For VaultMind, this paper is the biological grounding for the "pull-based" consolidation model: the cortex must be in the right state to receive hippocampal output. Consolidation is not pushed by the sender — it is gated by the receiver.
