# Mander et al. — Wake Deterioration and Sleep Restoration of Human Learning (2011)

Encoding capacity for new declarative information (face–name associations) degrades across a day of waking — hippocampal BOLD responses to new encoding events are lower in the afternoon than in the morning. A single night of sleep fully restored next-morning encoding capacity to morning levels. Using fMRI, the authors showed that the hippocampus is specifically restored: medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC) activity, which was elevated in the afternoon (possibly reflecting interference from saturated hippocampal buffers), returned to baseline after sleep.

The mechanistic interpretation is that slow-wave sleep clears hippocampal short-term buffers by transferring recent memories to neocortex, re-opening capacity for new encoding the following morning. This is the "preparation" function of sleep: not only does sleep consolidate what you learned, it prepares the brain to learn the next day.
