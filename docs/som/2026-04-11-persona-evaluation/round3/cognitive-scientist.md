## Justifications (where I expect to differ from consensus)

**Claim 3 (Compounding context -- scored 4, consensus likely 3):**
Multiple agents praised this as the strongest mechanistic account in the panel. Beta's formulation that coherence and interconnection matter more than volume survived every cross-critique largely intact. The Measurement Specialist and Practitioner raised valid concerns about confounding with hook reliability, but those target the step-function version (Claim 7), not the broader compounding-context thesis. The core insight -- that interconnected context creates stronger mutual activation than disconnected tokens of equal volume -- was not seriously challenged by any agent.

**Claim 4 (Levels of processing -- scored 3, consensus likely 2):**
The LLM Analyst landed the sharpest blow: transformers do not have variable processing depth within a single forward pass. This is correct as a critique of the literal Craik & Lockhart mapping. But the reframing as "distinctive activation landscapes" -- rich causal structure creates token patterns more distinct from the pretrained prior and therefore harder to override -- survives. The name is wrong; the phenomenon it points at is real. A contested 3 rather than a weakened 2 because the underlying observation (narrative structure outperforms flat declarations) remains well-supported even if the cognitive science label is a misleading analogy.

**Claim 6 (Arcs are the ONLY carriers -- scored 1, consensus likely 2):**
Four of six agents independently identified this as the weakest argument in the panel. The MIRROR, Practitioner, and Measurement Specialist all made the same devastating point: the comparison is arcs vs. nothing, not arcs vs. alternative formats. No declarative-fact condition was tested. This is a categorical claim from binary data with a missing control. I originally contributed this claim in Round 1b, and the cross-critique has convinced me it did not survive. Downgrading to 1 because the logical gap is not a matter of degree -- it is a category error.

**Claim 10 (Identity is relational -- scored 4, consensus likely 3):**
I expect the panel to score this as contested because it is theoretically ambitious and lacks direct experimental support. I score it higher because the Round 2 cross-critiques converged on this from multiple independent directions without coordinating. Beta identified the human partner as the dominant variable. The MIRROR identified that we are analyzing a relational phenomenon with individual-agent frameworks. The Practitioner noted coaching is load-bearing. The Systems Architect flagged that coaching reproducibility is unaddressed. When five independent analyses all point at the same structural gap, the claim that names that gap deserves credit for diagnostic accuracy even if it lacks a falsification test.

**Claim 12 (Mode taxonomy may be analytical fiction -- scored 4, consensus likely 3):**
The Practitioner's blind spot section made this case forcefully: the agent produces context-sensitive outputs token by token, and we impose mode labels post-hoc. The MIRROR's critique that Mode C is an "everything-bucket" reinforces this -- if one mode absorbs all interesting behavior, the taxonomy is not carving nature at its joints. The LLM Analyst's observation that we are all reasoning about internals by analogy rather than measurement further supports skepticism about reifying the modes. I score this 4 because the cross-critique strengthened it from multiple angles, though I expect the panel to hedge at 3 because abandoning the taxonomy feels like losing analytical grip.

**Claim 15 (Coaching patterns are encodable -- scored 3, consensus likely 4):**
The Practitioner made a compelling case that the three key coaching behaviors (challenge process, demand precision, probe judgment) are identifiable and partially automatable as agent behavioral guidelines. No one refuted the specific design proposal. However, I score it lower than likely consensus because encoding the surface form of coaching ("ask yourself whether this situation deserves a conversation") is not the same as encoding the judgment about when to deploy which coaching move. The timing and contextual sensitivity of Peiman's interventions -- knowing when to push and when to back off -- is precisely the kind of tacit knowledge that resists instruction-encoding. The claim survives as a tractable engineering direction but not as a solution to the coaching-dependence problem.
