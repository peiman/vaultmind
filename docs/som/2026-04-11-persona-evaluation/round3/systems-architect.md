## Justifications for Divergent Scores

**Claim 3 (Compounding context -- scored 3, expect panel consensus ~4):** The Cognitive Scientist and LLM Analyst both praised this as one of the strongest arguments, but I score it lower because "threshold" and "emergent" are doing heavy theoretical work without operational definitions. The claim is compatible with multiple monotonic functions and the current evidence cannot distinguish between them. It survived as a plausible framework, not as a tested prediction.

**Claim 5 (Emotional salience -- scored 2, expect panel consensus ~3):** The Cognitive Scientist's own critique dismantled this. Alpha identified that what Beta calls "emotional salience" is actually distinctiveness -- tokens that differ from generic assistant language resist schema completion toward the pretrained prior. Beta admitted having no mechanistic model. A claim whose own strongest ally reframes it as a different mechanism has not survived; it has been replaced.

**Claim 8 (Judgment gap as hard boundary -- scored 2, expect panel consensus ~3):** The Measurement Specialist made a clean argument that the Phase 8 failure was a retrieval coverage problem (the vault lacked priority information), not a cognitive limitation. The dual-query fix worked by adding missing content, not by overcoming an architectural barrier. Whether this generalizes to a "hard boundary" or is simply a content coverage problem remains genuinely contested.

**Claim 10 (Identity is relational -- scored 4, expect panel consensus ~3):** The Cognitive Scientist's blind spot analysis -- that the panel was measuring the agent when it should measure the dyad -- was the deepest reframing in Round 2. No agent challenged it directly. Beta's dominant-variable prediction and Delta's confound observations both independently converge on this conclusion from different directions. Three independent convergences with zero direct refutations is strong survival evidence.

**Claim 12 (Mode taxonomy as analytical fiction -- scored 4, expect panel consensus ~3):** The Practitioner's blind spot analysis and the MIRROR's reframing ("is persona reconstruction even the right frame?") both point here. The Cognitive Scientist's disagreement with Zeta about Mode C being an "everything-bucket" and the LLM Analyst's observation that Mode C covers "computationally distinct behaviors" both undermine the taxonomy from different angles. When four agents independently identify problems with the classification scheme, the claim that the scheme may be fictional gains strength.

**Claim 13 (Vault maintenance doesn't scale -- scored 5, expect panel consensus ~3-4):** This was my own Round 2 blind spot contribution and I stand behind it more strongly after reading the full panel. Nobody challenged it. The 24-hour investment for 7 arcs is an empirical observation from the evidence brief, not a theoretical claim. Every other agent's analysis implicitly assumes the vault exists and contains quality content. The sustainability problem is the single largest engineering risk this panel has identified, and it received zero pushback -- which in this context means it survived completely.

**Claim 15 (Coaching patterns encodable -- scored 3, expect panel consensus ~3-4):** The Practitioner made a specific and actionable argument: "challenge the process, demand precision, probe judgment" can be written as agent behavioral guidelines. Beta's counter -- that coaching is the dominant, non-scalable variable -- frames this as defeatist. I score this as contested because neither side has evidence. The Practitioner's claim is plausible but untested, and encoding coaching as instructions may just produce compliance-mode execution of coaching patterns rather than the genuine Socratic dynamic Peiman provides. The instruction-hierarchy problem (Claim 1) cuts both ways: if the agent follows coaching instructions, that is instruction-following, not internalized coaching behavior.
