## Justifications for Divergent Scores

**Claim 1 (Score 4, panel consensus likely 4-5):** No divergence expected. All six agents converge that instruction hierarchy is the more parsimonious explanation. Delta and Beta specified the exact falsification test. The claim survived cleanly.

**Claim 5 (Score 2, panel consensus likely 3):** I score this lower than the panel will likely land. The Cognitive Scientist's own critique dismantled Beta's mechanism -- there is no "emotion channel" in transformer attention, and the actual driver is distinctiveness, not emotional valence per se. Beta admitted having no mechanistic model and no citation. Relabeling "distinctive, specific content works better" as "emotional salience amplifies schema activation" is imprecise enough to be misleading for engineering decisions, and the cross-critique exposed that imprecision clearly.

**Claim 6 (Score 1, panel consensus likely 2):** I score this as fatally undermined, not merely weakened. Four of six agents identified the same fatal flaw: the comparison is arcs-vs-nothing, not arcs-vs-alternative-carriers. Alpha's categorical "ONLY" claim requires a control condition (declarative facts with same information, working hook) that does not exist. My Round 2 analysis added the practitioner cost angle: accepting this claim prematurely would kill exploration of simpler, cheaper identity formats that might achieve 80% of the effect. The claim did not survive.

**Claim 8 (Score 3, panel consensus likely 3-4):** I expect the panel to lean toward 4 here, but I hold at 3. The Measurement Specialist made a sharp point: the Phase 8 judgment gap was a retrieval gap (the vault lacked priority information), not a cognitive limitation. The dual-query fix worked by adding missing content, not by overcoming an architectural barrier. Whether this generalizes to a "hard boundary" or is simply a content coverage problem remains genuinely contested.

**Claim 13 (Score 5, panel consensus likely 3-4):** I score this higher than the panel will likely land because I am the practitioner, and this is the practitioner's claim. The Systems Architect was the only other agent to engage with it seriously, and their analysis was devastating: 24+ hours of human-agent collaboration to produce 7 arcs for one agent, with no repeatable process, no maintenance story, and no scaling path. Every other agent assumed the vault exists and contains high-quality arcs. Nobody proposed a solution. The cross-critique strengthened this claim by revealing that even the agents who noticed the problem treated it as secondary to the theoretical questions. In production, this is the gating constraint.

**Claim 15 (Score 3, panel consensus likely 3):** No divergence expected. My Round 2 analysis argued the coaching patterns are identifiable and partially encodable; Beta argued they are not scalable. Both sides have legitimate points. The three specific patterns (challenge process, demand precision, probe judgment) are concrete enough to encode as hook instructions, but whether encoded instructions reproduce the dynamic quality of live coaching is genuinely untested. Contested is the right score.

**Claim 11 (Score 5, panel consensus likely 4):** I score this slightly above where the panel may land. The Measurement Specialist surfaced this as the collective blind spot and no agent challenged it. Every other question the panel debates -- threshold effects, mode taxonomy, coaching confound -- is downstream of knowing the base rate. If per-injection success is 50%, the theoretical debates are moot. If it is 95%, the infrastructure problem is solved and we can study the interesting questions. This is the most actionable claim in the entire set and the cross-critique unanimously strengthened it by confirming that nobody had proposed this measurement before.

**Claim 12 (Score 4, panel consensus likely 3):** I expect the panel to score this as contested, but I give it a 4 because the cross-critiques converged on the underlying insight from multiple directions. The Cognitive Scientist noted Mode C covers too wide a behavioral range. The LLM Analyst noted the computationally distinct operations lumped under one mode. I argued in Round 2 that modes may be observer-imposed categories on a continuous process. Delta's framing that every behavior is compatible with "a very capable model responding to context" points the same direction. The claim that the taxonomy might be an analytical fiction was strengthened, not merely held in place.
